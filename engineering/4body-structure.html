Body Structure

Structural Blocks

The next step was to reinforce the bent sides with neck and tail blocks, and to attach kerfed lining to the edges to provide adequate gluing surface for the top and back.

The neck block is an important structural component that I wanted to be light, strong, and stable over time. I used scrap walnut and laminated a thin piece of plywood in between. This would theoretically improve the strength and stability of this component, especially in directions where the grain is weaker.

Neck block

Remaking the Sides

After gluing the neck and tail blocks on, I needed to thin the sides down to the final width. I set it up on a sled through the planer. All went well until the final pass, when I chipped the maple sides thinner than I wanted.

I could have went with a thinner body for the mandolin, but I was worried about it sounding to bright and harsh, so I decided to remake the sides and blocks. Thankfully I had more maple veneer, but I had to start from scratch, bending it again with a heat gun and then carefully laminating and carving the blocks to fit the sides perfectly.

Sides with body blocks

Planer sled that failed

Kerfed Lining

The kerfed lining is critical for a strong glue joint between the sides and the top and back plates. It is basically just a thin triangular strip of wood with a bunch of slits — the slides allow it to flex to the shape of the sides. With the sides in the mold to hold the shape perfectly, I glued each section of kerfing to the edge, clamping it down with clothes pins reinforced with rubber bands.
