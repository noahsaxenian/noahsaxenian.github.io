Apple Cider Press

Fabrication Machining Manufacturing Metal Parts

This benchtop apple cider press was our group final project for a Materials and Manufacturing course.

Group members: Eli Singer, Ryan Cole, Ben Fisher, and Nik Rattan

Working Video

Completed press

Components

The frame was constructed using wood and sheet metal brackets. I manufactured the top plate out of 1/2 inch steel by first cutting to length on the bandsaw and then drilling and tapping each of the holes.

During first tests of the press, the bottom plate flexed significantly under load — a major design flaw. I realized we could stiffen the bottom easily by screwing 2x4's underneath.

Frame construction

I cut the shaft from a length of 7/8" threaded rod. I used the lathe to turn the threads off the bottom and sharpened it to a point to reduce friction with the press block. We sandwiched the top aluminum square between several nuts to keep it secure. The bolts sticking out the top allow for any long and stiff item to be used for mechanical advantage.

Shaft detail

I cut the press plate by hand from a sheet of polycarbonate. I fabricated the aluminum block to be attached with screws to the plate. The hole in the top accepts the pointed shaft so that the press does not spin as the shaft is turned.

Press plate and aluminum block

Other group members fabricated the barrel from thin aluminum sheet metal, using a punch to make the holes and securing it into a cylinder with rivets.

Sheet metal barrel with punched holes

Other group members made the collection tray using aluminum sheet metal and rivets. Ultimately, silicone was needed to seal the gaps.

Collection tray

Final Thoughts

Many improvements could be made to this design for a future version. Firstly, the frame is poorly designed. Simply extending the side supports below the bottom plate would allow for secure and easy mounting of 2x4's underneath, greatly increasing the overall stiffness of the frame and allowing it to take more force. Additionally, diagonal braces would be a useful addition to the frame as it has a tendency to rack under large loads. The collection tray is another area for improvement, as the current design leaks without the aid of silicone. A rivet-less, deformed design could be a way to minimize leaks.

We noticed the steel bar on top deforming during our tests. With a rough measurement of displacement and some calculations we determined a max load of around 6kN.

This project was a great demonstration of fabrication techniques we learned throughout the course as well as an opportunity for me to pursue my interest in more advanced techniques such as using the manual mill and lathe. The result was a working press, that we used to make and enjoy several cups of apple cider.
